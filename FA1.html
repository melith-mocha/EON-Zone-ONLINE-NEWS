Why Low Waistlines Are Back in Fashion

By: Arpita Hota

Published: October 28, 2025

The devil may wear Prada, but right now, she's definitely wearing it with a dropped waistline

There’s a tectonic shift happening at the waistline – it’s slipping lower than we’ve seen in decades. The low-waistline dress is having a moment – no, scratch that – it's having the moment. After years of high-waisted tyranny, jeans, skirts, trousers all inching north, the low-waistline silhouette feels like a surprisingly fresh breath of air tinged with nostalgia. Designers are reviving the 1920s flapper silhouette with a modern sensibility – unruly in spirit, achingly romantic in form.

Spring 2024 catwalks signalled this shift unmistakably. Sandy Liang's whimsical interpretations featured pleated pinafores and mesh maxis adorned with delicate flower belts, while Schiaparelli delivered glamorous draped designs with bubbling skirts that commanded attention. The message was clear: the waistline was migrating south, and fashion was following with enthusiasm.

What makes this trend particularly compelling is its versatility. Unlike the restrictive corsetry that defined previous eras, today's low-waistline dresses embrace comfort without sacrificing elegance. Whether rendered in crisp cotton for daytime ease or sheer lace for evening glamour, these designs offer an effortless sophistication that modern women crave. The silhouette is inherently forgiving, skimming over the midsection while drawing attention to the legs and creating an elongated, graceful line.

Celebrities have turned this runway revival into a full-blown red-carpet moment. At the 2024 Met Gala, lowered waistlines reigned supreme – Ariana Grande stunned in a strapless Loewe number with dramatic drop-waist pleating, and Taylor Russell took a sculptural turn in another Loewe creation featuring a wood-effect basque that stole the spotlight. Later that night, Kendall Jenner kept the mood going at the afterparty in archival Givenchy by Alexander McQueen, proving the silhouette moves effortlessly from high glamour to after-hours allure.

The momentum continued throughout the year. Zendaya and Lady Gaga have both championed the drop-waist aesthetic in their style evolution, while Kylie Jenner and Sofia Richie have seamlessly woven the trend into their wardrobes, making it feel both aspirational and accessible. As we entered the latter half of 2024 and now 2025, the trend has only intensified. Dakota Johnson reimagined the naked dress concept at the 2025 Zurich Film Festival in a cobalt blue Gucci gown with a low Basque-style waistline that exploded into voluminous tulle, while Kirsten Dunst channeled pure 1930s elegance in a drop-waist Gucci dress at the 2025 Golden Globes.

The timing couldn't be more perfect. This latter part of the year has seen fashion playing gleefully with nostalgia, as Y2K aesthetics continue their stranglehold on the cultural zeitgeist and low-rise bottoms reclaim their place in our collective wardrobe consciousness. The drop-waist dress feels like the natural, fitted evolution of this nostalgic revival – a way to embrace lowered proportions with elegance rather than the more casual approach of early-aughts denim. It's vintage inspiration filtered through a contemporary lens, honouring the past while firmly looking forward.

Beyond the red carpet, the democratisation has been swift and comprehensive. Brands from House of CB to Reformation, Lulus to Abercrombie have embraced the silhouette, offering interpretations at every price point. High street retailers have made this more than a runway fantasy, transforming it into a wearable reality for fashion enthusiasts everywhere. The trend is also being heralded as a key bridal direction for 2025, with its romantic silhouette and vintage-inspired proportions perfectly suited to modern brides seeking alternatives to traditional conventions.
